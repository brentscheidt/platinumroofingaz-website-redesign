Insurance Claims

Filing a roof damage insurance claim in Arizona.

Arizona homeowners have strong claim rights after hail and wind events. The process has more moving parts than most people expect — here's how to navigate it without leaving money on the table.

Step 1: Document before anything else is touched

The most common mistake homeowners make is calling a roofer before documenting the damage themselves. Your documentation matters independently of what your roofer produces — it establishes your perspective as the property owner and creates a timestamped record that predates any contractor involvement.

Immediately after a storm event, photograph and video the following:

  • The exterior of the roof from all four sides, from ground level
  • Any visible damage on the surface — displaced tiles, dented metal, missing shingles
  • Interior ceilings and attic spaces showing any water staining, wet insulation, or active drips
  • Any exterior property damage that confirms storm activity: dented gutters, damaged AC units, dented vehicles, stripped tree branches
  • Storm event context: NWS alerts on your phone, weather app screenshots, local news reports

All photos should have location data enabled. Note the date, time, and a brief description of what each image shows. The more detailed this documentation, the harder it is for an adjuster to dispute the event or its severity.

Step 2: Contact your carrier and open the claim

File as soon as you have documentation in hand — ideally within 24–48 hours of the event. Most homeowner policies have reporting requirements, and delayed filing can complicate your claim even when damage is clear. When you call, keep it factual: date and time of the event, what you observed, and that you're requesting an adjuster inspection. Don't speculate about total damage or repair cost at this stage.

Ask your carrier for your claim number, the name of the adjuster assigned, and a clear timeline for their inspection. Write all of this down. In Arizona's post-monsoon season, adjusters are handling dozens of claims simultaneously — following up within a week if you haven't heard back is reasonable and expected.

Step 3: Get a contractor inspection before the adjuster arrives

Having a roofing contractor inspect and document damage before the insurance adjuster visits is one of the most important things you can do. A qualified roofer knows what hail damage, wind uplift, and impact damage looks like at the material level — and they can identify damage that's easy to miss from the ground or during a quick adjuster walkthrough.

What your roofer should provide after their inspection:

  • Written scope of damage: Line-item description of every damaged component — field tiles, ridge caps, flashings, underlayment, gutters, HVAC surrounds
  • Measurements: Accurate roof square footage by slope and section, plus linear footage of ridge, hips, valleys, and eaves
  • Photo documentation: Roof-level photos of impact marks, displacement patterns, and any exposed underlayment
  • Material identification: Manufacturer, product line, and color match information for any materials that need replacement

This scope becomes your reference document when comparing against the adjuster's estimate. Discrepancies between the two are common and worth addressing before you accept any settlement.

Step 4: The adjuster inspection

Be present for the adjuster inspection. You're not required to be, but your presence allows you to point out damage that might be missed, reference your contractor's scope, and ask questions in real time. If your roofer is available to be on-site simultaneously, that's even better — a contractor walking the roof with the adjuster produces better outcomes than a homeowner standing on the ground watching.

After the inspection, the adjuster will produce an estimate typically within 5–10 business days. Review it against your contractor's scope before signing or accepting anything.

Common mistakes that reduce claim payouts

  • Accepting the first estimate without comparison: Adjuster estimates frequently omit line items — secondary water damage barriers, code-required upgrades, permit fees, and matching material costs are commonly missed
  • Signing a direction-of-benefits agreement before understanding the scope: These transfer your claim rights to the contractor and remove your ability to negotiate
  • Completing repairs before the adjuster inspects: Emergency tarping is appropriate; replacing materials before adjuster sign-off can complicate or void parts of your claim
  • Not accounting for depreciation vs. replacement cost value: ACV (actual cash value) policies pay less than RCV (replacement cost value) policies — know which you have before accepting a settlement

Timeline expectations

A straightforward Arizona claim typically moves on this schedule: file within 48 hours, adjuster inspection within 1–2 weeks, initial estimate within 2 weeks of inspection, supplement resolution within 2–4 weeks if needed, and repairs authorized within 30–45 days of filing. Major storm events that hit multiple zip codes simultaneously can add 2–4 weeks at each stage. Complex claims with supplements or disputes run 60–90 days or longer.

When to push back on a denial or low estimate

If the adjuster's estimate is significantly lower than your contractor's scope, or if your claim is denied, you have recourse. First, request a written explanation from your carrier. Then provide your contractor's scope and documentation as a formal supplement request. If the gap remains after one round, you can request a re-inspection with a field supervisor, invoke your policy's appraisal clause (which brings in a neutral appraiser), or consult a licensed public adjuster. In Arizona, the Department of Insurance handles consumer complaints and can intervene if a carrier is not handling your claim in good faith.
