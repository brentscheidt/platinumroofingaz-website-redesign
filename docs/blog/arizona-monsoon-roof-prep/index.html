Monsoon Prep

Arizona monsoon season roof prep.

Monsoon season runs June through September in Arizona. What you do in May and early June determines whether a haboob or microburst becomes a minor inconvenience or a major insurance claim.

Why June is the right window for inspection

Arizona's monsoon season officially begins June 15th and runs through September 30th. That means May and early June is your inspection window — enough time to complete repairs before the first storm rolls in. Scheduling after the season starts means competing with dozens of other homeowners who waited, and it means the damage you missed will likely compound during the first event.

Pre-monsoon inspection is also strategically important for insurance documentation. If damage exists before monsoon season and you don't document it, your carrier may dispute whether storm damage is new or pre-existing. A timestamped inspection report from June protects you in September when you need to file.

Pre-monsoon inspection checklist

Walk through these areas before June 15th, either yourself from the ground or with a contractor on the roof:

Flashing at all penetrations

Check around HVAC curbs, pipes, skylights, and chimneys. Arizona's thermal cycling — 115°F days cooling to 70°F at night — causes flashing to expand and contract hundreds of times per year. Sealant cracks and metal separates. Look for gaps, lifted edges, or dried-out caulk. These are the most common monsoon leak entry points.

Valleys and drainage paths

Arizona monsoons drop intense rain in short windows — 1–3 inches in under an hour is common in the Phoenix metro. Valleys and drainage paths that are partially blocked by debris, failed mortar, or displaced tiles will funnel water in ways the roof wasn't designed to handle. Clear all debris and confirm valley metal is intact and sealed at edges.

Tile displacement and cracked tiles

High winds precede most monsoon rain events. Tiles that are already loose or cracked can be displaced before rain even arrives, opening the underlayment to direct water exposure. Walk the perimeter and look for any tiles that have shifted, lifted at edges, or show visible cracks. On a steep slope, use binoculars rather than climbing — and leave the roof walk to a contractor.

Foam and flat roof coating condition

SPF (spray polyurethane foam) roofs require periodic recoating to maintain waterproofing. If your foam roof's topcoat is cracked, blistered, or showing bare foam, it will absorb water during a monsoon event and the damage compounds quickly. Check the surface for visible coating failure before June.

Gutters and scuppers

Blocked drainage forces water to pool, which accelerates any existing weakness into an active leak. Clean gutters completely and confirm scupper openings on flat roofs are clear. Monsoon debris loads are significant — palm fronds, dust, and gravel can block a scupper in a single event.

How to document existing damage for insurance

Before monsoon season, photograph your entire roof systematically: each elevation from the ground, close-ups of any visible damage, and interior shots of any existing water staining in the attic or ceiling. Date-stamp everything. If you have a roofer inspect, get a written condition report with their observations and the date. This documentation becomes your baseline — it proves what was pre-existing versus what the storm caused, and it protects you from carrier disputes about whether hail or wind damage is new.

When to call a contractor vs. handle it yourself

DIY is reasonable for: clearing debris from gutters and valleys, replacing individual broken tiles on accessible low-slope sections if you're comfortable on a roof, and applying small amounts of roofing sealant to isolated caulk gaps. Call a contractor for: anything involving flashing replacement or resealing around penetrations, foam roof evaluation or recoating, any damage you can't see or reach safely, and anything you suspect may involve the underlayment beneath the surface material.

In Arizona's summer heat, roof surface temperatures exceed 160°F by mid-morning. Any inspection or repair work should happen before 8am. This is not a "push through it" situation — heat illness risk on a roof in June is real.

Emergency tarp procedures after a storm event

If a monsoon event causes active damage and you can't get a contractor on-site immediately, tarping limits interior damage while you wait. Use a minimum 6-mil poly tarp, extend it past the damaged area on all sides, and weight or secure edges at the ridge and eaves. Do not nail or staple a tarp into the roofing material — it creates additional penetrations. Use sandbags or weight boards along edges. Never go on a wet roof during or immediately after a storm.
