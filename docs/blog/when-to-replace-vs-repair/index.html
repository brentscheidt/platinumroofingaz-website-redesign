Roofing Advice

When to replace vs. repair your roof.

Age, leak history, and damage patterns all factor in. Here's how Arizona roofers think through the decision — and when each option actually saves you money.

Start with age — it sets the baseline

The first question isn't "how bad is the damage?" — it's "how old is the roof?" Age changes the math on everything. A 10-year-old tile roof with a few cracked tiles is almost always worth repairing. A 22-year-old tile roof with those same cracks may not be.

In Arizona, general lifespan benchmarks for the most common roofing systems are:

  • Concrete tile: 15–25 years before underlying underlayment starts failing, even if tiles look fine from the street
  • Asphalt shingle: 20–25 years in moderate climates, but often 15–18 in the Phoenix metro due to intense UV and heat cycling
  • Flat/foam (SPF): 15–20 years depending on recoat history and foot traffic
  • Metal: 40–70 years with minimal maintenance

Once a roof is within 5 years of its expected end of life, every repair dollar you spend is borrowed time. You're patching a system that's already failing at the substrate level — the tiles, shingles, or coating may hold, but the felt, underlayment, and flashings underneath are degrading.

Leak frequency is the clearest signal

One leak in five years: repair it. One leak a year for three years in the same area: repair it carefully and monitor. Two or more leaks in different locations in the same season: you're managing a failing system, not isolated damage.

Multiple leak points mean the underlying waterproofing is compromised across the roof, not just at one penetration or seam. At that stage, each repair buys months, not years — and the cumulative cost of repeated repairs often exceeds the cost of replacement within 2–3 cycles.

Visible damage patterns that point toward replacement

Not all damage is equal. Some damage is cosmetic or isolated. Other patterns indicate systemic failure:

  • Granule loss on shingles: Normal over time, but heavy granule loss across the whole field means the surface is done. Spot-replacing shingles won't fix a roof that's baked past its useful life.
  • Widespread tile displacement: Arizona monsoons can shift tiles. A few displaced tiles after a storm is normal. Dozens across multiple sections, or recurring displacement every season, points to a fastening or underlayment problem that surface work won't solve.
  • Sagging or soft spots: Any structural deformation — sagging decking, soft spots when walked on, visible dips from the ground — indicates wood rot or structural damage that requires more than a patch.
  • Flashing deterioration on most penetrations: Flashings around chimneys, HVAC curbs, skylights, and valleys degrade at roughly the same rate. If multiple flashings are failing simultaneously, the whole system is at end of life.

When repair is the smarter call

Repair makes clear sense when: the roof is under 15 years old, damage is isolated to one section or penetration, the underlying decking is dry and solid, and you're not seeing multiple simultaneous failure points. Storm damage on an otherwise healthy roof is the ideal repair scenario — the damage is defined, the cause is known, and the rest of the system is intact. Insurance often covers storm damage repairs specifically, which makes the economics even more straightforward.

When replacement saves money long-term

Replacement becomes the better financial decision when repair costs exceed 30–40% of replacement cost, when the roof is approaching or past its expected lifespan, or when you're planning to sell within 3–5 years and a failing roof will surface in inspection. In Arizona's real estate market, a new roof adds measurable resale value and removes a significant buyer objection.

Arizona's climate accelerates wear. UV intensity here is roughly equivalent to a second decade of aging compared to northern climates. A shingle roof that would last 25 years in Colorado may genuinely be done at 18 years in Phoenix. Factor local conditions, not national averages, into your decision.

The insurance angle

If your roof has storm damage — hail impacts, wind displacement, or impact marks — insurance can shift the entire repair-vs-replace calculation. A roof that might be a borderline replacement candidate on age alone could qualify for a full replacement if a qualifying storm event caused enough damage across the field. This is worth knowing before you decide to fund a repair out-of-pocket. Have your roof inspected by a contractor familiar with insurance documentation before you commit to either path.
